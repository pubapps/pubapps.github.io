    There’s a probably a big problem with your 401(K)—mysterious fees

    Yahoo FinanceNovember 6, 2016

    By Bob Ward, Chief Revenue Officer of Vertical Management Systems

    Everyone in a retirement plan is writing a check. They just don’t know for how much.

    American workers have over $4 trillion in company 401(k) plans. In spite of the fact that there are more than 500,000 plans in force with about 52 million participating employees, there is a serious problem: Most people have no idea how much their plan actually costs them.

    “Cost,” you wonder? Like many employees, you may be under the impression that your 401(k) plan is a free company benefit. And it’s no wonder. 401(k) plan providers do not make it easy to find out what fees employees pay to participate in the plan.

    In 2012, the United States Department of Labor (DOL) mandated that 401(k) plans disclose all fees associated with plan administration, investments and other expenses. But according to a 2013 study by Personal Capital, a year after the ruling “… 22 percent of participants believed they didn’t pay fees and expenses [even] after receiving disclosure notices….”

    Who you gonna call?

    Recently, a friend of mine, who works for a large, national financial services company, contacted the company’s 401(k) plan to ask a simple question:  How much does my retirement plan cost?

    Here is a brief recap of the conversation.

    My friend:  Can you tell me how much my 401(k) plan costs?

    Representative: Nothing, it’s free.

    My friend:  It’s free? There is absolutely no cost to me?

    Representative: Well, yes, there are some costs, but there is no charge for participating in the plan.

    My friend:  Can you tell me what fees and expenses I am paying?

    Representative:  If you go to our website, you will find information on our plan fees and investment expenses. I’m afraid I cannot provide you with your specific costs.

    Did you say “free” or “fee”?

    While the DOL mandated years ago that employees like my friend should not have to work so hard to understand the costs associated with saving for retirement, the situation remains relatively unchanged. Considering all the talk about America’s retirement crisis, why is it so hard to get answers to a basic question like, “How much does my retirement plan cost?”

    The answer may surprise you. The majority of companies that offer 401(k) plans simply do not have the capability to tell individual employees what they pay in fees and expenses. Believe it or not, it’s a technology problem.

    That’s why the company representative in my friend’s case, directed her to the website. She was expected to do her own research—into the  company’s plan—in order to find out how much it costs. And the task proved formidable. She had to sort through complex tables full of fine print and basically try to guesstimate how much of her regular contributions went towards saving for retirement and how much went to fees and expenses.

    It’s not that companies are trying to be secretive or purposely withhold information from their employees. The problem is that technology hasn’t kept up with changing regulations and consumer protections.

    In a new ruling issued in April 2016, the Department of Labor increased the onus on retirement plan sponsors to act in the best interests of plan participants. Fee transparency is a big component of this. Once companies upgrade their technology, they should be able to provide employees with the level of information on plan fees they deserve.

    How to solve the mystery

    There are justifiable costs associated with anything we buy, including a 401(k) plan. The plans rely on a network of service providers. Each plays a vital role in making the plan work, and each charges the plan for its services. In the end, it’s the plan’s participants who pay these fees. In return, they should be told exactly what those fees are.

    In addition to a plan’s administrative costs, there are fees associated with the specific investments each participant selects. Mutual funds—the primary investments people hold in their 401(k) plans—charge their own set of fees. (See below.)

    Do not be fooled by these seemingly tiny numbers. As of the end of 2012, participants in their sixties with more than 30 years of tenure at their current employer had an average 401(k) account balance of $224,287!!

    Conclusion

    We wouldn’t take a mortgage on our house without knowing the interest rate. Yet, there is much we do not know about the costs associated with a 401(k) plan.

    The good news is that regulators are beginning to hold retirement plan sponsors accountable for the fees they charge. And they are insisting that HR departments and plan representatives take steps to improve employee communications and fee transparency.

    We’re in the midst of important creative destruction. It’s time for the industry to evolve and for 401(k) plans to make required technology fixes in order to comply with current regulations. Until they do, employees with 401(k) plans are just going to have to do their own research.
